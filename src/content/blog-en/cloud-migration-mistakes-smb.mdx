Cloud migration is often sold as a cure-all: lower costs, greater flexibility, instant access to cutting-edge technology. Yet for many small and medium-sized businesses across Canada and the US, the journey turns out bumpier than expected. After guiding dozens of organizations through digital transformation, the Agilion team has identified five mistakes that come up again and again — and that carry a significant price tag.

## Mistake #1: Migrating Without Evaluating What Actually Belongs in the Cloud

The most common reflex is wanting to migrate everything at once. Internal applications, databases, file servers, virtual desktops — the temptation to move it all to AWS or Azure in a single wave is real.

### The problem

Some applications were built to run on-premises hardware and aren't architected for the cloud. Adapting them often costs more than the benefits gained. Others are nearing end-of-life and should simply be replaced rather than migrated.

### The solution

Start with a rigorous application inventory. Classify each system by cloud compatibility, strategic value, and the cost of migration versus replacement. Prioritize applications that will genuinely benefit from cloud scalability and high availability — not everything deserves to go.

## Mistake #2: Ignoring Recurring Costs

One of the most persistent myths: "the cloud is cheaper." Sometimes it is — but not automatically.

### The problem

Organizations compare their physical infrastructure costs (servers amortized over five years) against monthly cloud rates, without factoring in data transfer fees, additional licensing, technical support, and the human resources required to manage the new infrastructure.

### The solution

Model your costs over three years before you start. Include bandwidth fees, redundant storage, backups, and the time your IT team will spend on cloud administration. Tools like the AWS Pricing Calculator or Azure Cost Management can help you project realistic numbers.

## Mistake #3: Underestimating Security and Compliance Requirements

Moving to the cloud doesn't transfer security responsibility away from you. This is called the shared responsibility model — and many SMBs learn this the hard way.

### The problem

Amazon, Microsoft, and Google secure the physical infrastructure and hypervisors. But your instance configuration, access management, data encryption, and compliance with applicable privacy laws remain entirely your responsibility. In Canada, PIPEDA and provincial legislation like Quebec's Law 25 impose specific obligations on how you handle personal data.

### The solution

Build security in from the start — security by design. Enable multi-factor authentication for all cloud access, encrypt data at rest and in transit, and establish access policies based on the principle of least privilege. For businesses with regulatory obligations, have your architecture validated by a specialist before go-live.

## Mistake #4: Underestimating the Learning Curve

AWS alone offers more than 200 distinct services. Azure is comparable. Mastering these environments takes time, training, and sustained effort.

### The problem

SMBs often assume their existing IT team will adapt naturally. In reality, effectively managing cloud infrastructure requires specific skills: Infrastructure as Code (IaC), cloud cost management, cloud security, monitoring, and observability. Without these competencies, your infrastructure quickly becomes disorganized and expensive.

### The solution

Invest in training before and during the migration. Certifications like AWS Solutions Architect Associate or Microsoft Azure Fundamentals provide a solid foundation. Alternatively, partnering with an experienced firm like Agilion for the initial phases enables gradual knowledge transfer to your internal team.

## Mistake #5: Not Planning a Rollback Strategy

Nobody wants to think about failure when planning a migration — but the absence of a contingency plan is one of the most dangerous oversights.

### The problem

A poorly planned migration can disrupt operations for days. If you haven't maintained your on-premises infrastructure in parallel while validating the migration, you have no exit route if a critical problem emerges.

### The solution

Adopt a phased migration approach with clear validation gates. Keep your existing infrastructure operational for at least 30 to 60 days after each migration wave. Define measurable success criteria and a rollback trigger threshold before you begin — not in the middle of a crisis.

## Conclusion

A successful cloud migration isn't a technical project — it's a business transformation. It requires rigorous planning, careful change management, and solid technical expertise.
